# Vocabulary Search as a Research Method

## Moving from Frequency Trends Back to Usage

This handout explains how to turn a word search into a reproducible research method. The goal is not to treat a frequency graph as proof of semantic change, but to use it to formulate a question and then return to dated examples, dictionary evidence, corpus contexts, and the literary text.

## 1. Decide Before You Search

Before entering a word, decide:

1. Which word or words are you studying?
2. What exactly are you comparing?
3. Are the comparison conditions consistent?

Record spelling, part of speech, date range, corpus, smoothing, case sensitivity, and any filters. A change in one condition may change the result.

## 2. Three Comparison Patterns

### A. Compare the Same Word Across Time

Keep the word and corpus fixed while changing the period.

Examples:

- `freedom`, 1800–1900, English
- `anxiety`, 1800–2000, American English
- `comfort`, 1800–1900, English Fiction

After identifying a rise, decline, or peak, read examples from the relevant periods. Frequency change alone does not demonstrate meaning change.

### B. Compare Related Words Under the Same Conditions

Keep the date range and corpus fixed while comparing words with related meanings.

Examples:

- `freedom` and `liberty`, 1800–1900, English
- `honest`, `sincere`, and `earnest`, 1800–1900, English Fiction
- `fear`, `terror`, and `anxiety`, 1800–1900, English

Whenever possible, compare words with the same part of speech. Similar words may behave differently in actual usage.

### C. Compare the Same Word Across Corpora

Keep the word fixed while comparing corpora such as American English, British English, or English Fiction.

Examples:

- `home` across English, American English, British English, and English Fiction
- `labor` and `labour`, with spelling and regional variation considered explicitly

Corpora differ in size, genre, region, and selection policy. A difference between corpora may reflect the materials collected rather than a difference in meaning.

## 3. Basic Workflow

1. Choose a target word and identify its part of speech.
2. Select comparison pattern A, B, or C.
3. Use [Google Ngram Viewer](https://books.google.com/ngrams) with a consistent date range, corpus, and smoothing setting.
4. Identify one period that deserves closer attention.
5. Use [Google Books Advanced Search](https://books.google.com/advanced_book_search?hl=en) to read examples from that period.
6. Consult the [Oxford English Dictionary](https://www.oed.com/) or another historical dictionary for senses and dated quotations.
7. Use a corpus such as [COHA](https://www.english-corpora.org/coha/), [CLiC Fiction](https://clic-fiction.com/), or another appropriate collection to inspect concordance lines and genre-specific contexts.
8. Return to the course text with DH Reading Lab or [Voyant Tools](https://voyant-tools.org/) and inspect frequency, KWIC, and distribution within the work.
9. Separate what the frequency trend suggests from what the usage examples actually show.
10. Record what remains uncertain and formulate the next question.

## 4. What Each Tool Contributes

### Google Ngram Viewer

Use it to observe long-term changes in the relative frequency of words or phrases in Google Books. Record the query, date range, corpus, smoothing, case setting, and URL. Treat the graph as an entry point, not as a conclusion about meaning.

### Google Books Advanced Search

Use it to return from a frequency trend to books published in a specific period. Check genre, publication date, edition, OCR quality, and the surrounding passage.

### Historical Dictionaries

Use the OED or another historical dictionary to distinguish senses and examine dated quotations. A dictionary history can help test whether a frequency change corresponds to a change in usage.

### Historical and Literary Corpora

Use COHA for historical American English and CLiC Fiction for searchable nineteenth-century fiction. Corpus design matters: note region, period, genre, size, and access conditions.

### DH Reading Lab and Voyant Tools

Use these tools to return to the literary text itself. Inspect where the word occurs, how it appears in KWIC context, whether it clusters in particular scenes, and whether the larger historical pattern is relevant to this specific work.

## 5. Source Selection

Choose sources according to the question rather than collecting as many as possible.

| Research need | Useful starting point | Main caution |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Long-term publication trend | Google Ngram Viewer | A graph does not explain meaning |
| Dated examples from books | Google Books | Check OCR, edition, and publication metadata |
| Historical American English | COHA | Registration and usage limits may apply |
| Nineteenth-century fiction contexts | CLiC Fiction | It is a literary corpus, not all British English |
| A course text or small corpus | DH Reading Lab or Voyant Tools | Results describe the texts you supplied |
| Historical senses and quotations | OED or another historical dictionary | Library access may be required |
| Plain-text literary works | Project Gutenberg | Check edition and editorial history |
| Page images and OCR from specific editions | Internet Archive or HathiTrust | Access and OCR quality vary by item |

## 6. Example Research Design

Question: Did `freedom` and `liberty` function in the same way in nineteenth-century English-language texts?

- Words: `freedom`, `liberty`
- Part of speech: nouns
- Period: 1800–1900
- Corpus: English
- Comparison: related words under the same conditions

First compare the trends. Then select periods where the difference is noticeable, read dated examples, consult dictionary senses, and inspect how the words appear in the literary texts studied in class. The final claim should distinguish frequency, historical usage, and interpretation of the specific work.

## 8. Goal

A strong conclusion separates observation from interpretation:

> I compared `_____` with `_____` in the `_____` corpus for the period `_____`. The frequency data suggested `_____`, but the usage examples showed `_____`. Returning to the literary text raised the further question `_____`.

The objective is not a perfect answer. It is a transparent path from search conditions, to evidence, to a more precise literary question.
