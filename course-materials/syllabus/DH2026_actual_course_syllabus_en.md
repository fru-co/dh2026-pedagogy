# How to Go Digital in American Literary Studies

**Spring 2026 · Kohei Furuya**

## Course Description

This class is designed for students (and a teacher) who are entirely new to Digital Humanities. There has been a great deal of discussion about the pros and cons of using machines to study literary texts. Consciously ignorant of—and secretly scared by—the incredible scale and speed of recent developments in NLP, LLMs, and related technologies, traditional humanities scholars (including myself) have often cast a suspicious eye on these new tools and tried to convince themselves that machines cannot really “read” texts.

The advent of generative AI, however, makes it increasingly difficult to maintain that comforting belief. Like something that quietly sneaks into everyday life—almost like a virus—AI is already among us. In our classrooms and on the internet, we are witnessing a sudden explosion of AI-generated texts, and there seems to be no way out of this rapidly accelerating digital environment. Nobody can tell anymore whether a text was written by a human or by an AI, and the air of mutual distrust—or perhaps tacit complicity—between students and teachers is hard to deny. This may sound like a science-fiction story, but it is very much a real one.

In this class, we will read literary texts primarily from nineteenth-century American literature, the instructor’s field of specialization, while experimenting with computational approaches to literary analysis, including n-grams, Word2Vec, BERT, and generative AI. Students will work with very small-scale methods, such as identifying repeated phrases and exploring clusters of meaning around key words, and, if possible, engage in vibe coding—writing simple code, building small apps, and sharing what they have made with the class.

Each class session will typically combine close reading, shared annotation on Google Docs, and a short hands-on experiment with a digital tool.

All primary texts will be shared through Google Docs. Students are expected to add comments throughout the semester. These may focus on close reading and interpretation or propose ideas for digital or computational ways of analyzing the text.

Theoretical approaches are always appreciated. If you want to write a fifty-page philosophical critique of AI, you are free to do so. But the main goal of this course—whether you like it or not—is to DO SOMETHING with digital tools. This may involve platforms like Codex and Claude Code. We might end up leaving behind a huge mass of mere digital trash, but even that would be more valuable than producing an elegant theoretical observation about the relationship between the digital and the human without ever touching the tools themselves.

The course values process over results, and even failed experiments or messy outputs are treated as meaningful outcomes.

Students who have been hesitant or reluctant to use digital methods in their studies—myself included—are especially welcome. Students who are already interested in digital approaches are, of course, welcome as well.

## Course Method

Students will give at least one presentation and submit comments for each class; these comments will form the basis of discussion. A final paper is due at the end of the semester.

## Grading

Grades will be based on participation in weekly in-class activities, at least one presentation, and a final paper that reflects on the student’s work and outcomes produced during the course.

- In-class activities and contributions: 30%
- Presentation: 20%
- Final paper (textual analysis and report on digital experiments): 50%

The presentation and final paper do not require technical sophistication. Students should reflect on what they attempted, what worked, what failed, and what they learned.
