DH2026 Week 12 Handout

Title

Thomas Bangs Thorpe, "The Big Bear of Arkansas"

1. Today's Theme

This week we read Thomas Bangs Thorpe's "The Big Bear of Arkansas" and return to the DH tools we have used so far. Because we again had limited time for hands-on app work last week, this class prioritizes practice: reading the text, searching, observing results, and returning to the text.

2. What Matters This Week

1. The text depends on exaggeration and regional performance

"The Big Bear of Arkansas" is set on a steamboat, where a man from Arkansas performs exaggerated stories about his land, hunting, bears, and nature. The question is not only what happened, but how the story is being told.

2. This week compares methods

So far, we have used frequency analysis, KWIC, word vectors, HistWords, and SBERT Text Lab. This week we apply multiple methods to the same text and ask which method fits which kind of question.

3. Tool results can become material for the final paper

In the final paper, you will discuss at least two texts and use multiple tools. Today's experiment memo can become part of your work log and may also lead directly into your argument.

3. Today's Questions

  1. How does "The Big Bear of Arkansas" construct regional or frontier identity through narration?
  2. In what contexts do words such as "Arkansaw," "stranger," "bear," "game," "natur," and "hunt" appear?
  3. Are exaggeration, humor, and violence easy to find through search, or do they require close reading?
  4. What do keyword search, word-vector search, and SBERT semantic search each find well, and what does each miss?
  5. How can this text be compared with Poe, Irving, Paulding, McCune Smith, Stowe, or other texts we have read?

4. What We Will Do Today

  1. Share comments
  2. Review the structure of the text, the tall tale, the steamboat setting, and regional humor
  3. Use Google Docs, DH Reading Lab, or Voyant to examine repeated words and contexts
  4. Use SBERT Text Lab for semantic search within the text
  5. If time allows, try one additional tool: Word Vector Explorer, HistWords Explorer, or Historical BERT Explorer
  6. Write a short experiment memo that can connect to the final paper
