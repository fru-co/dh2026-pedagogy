# DH2026 Week 8 Handout

## Title

Nathaniel Parker Willis, "The Lunatic's Skate"

## 1. Today's Theme

This week we read "The Lunatic's Skate" and ask how descriptions of movement, skating, and madness create a sense of speed. Rather than adding a new technical method, we return to the methods we have already used: frequency, distribution, nearby words, and relations between words, and connect them to rhythm and motion in the text.

## 2. What Matters This Week

### 1. Felt speed comes from style as well as content

A "fast" scene does not feel fast only because characters are moving quickly. Verbs, sentence length, punctuation, repetition, and shifts of perspective all help create the speed and tension of reading.

### 2. Compare numbers with felt experience

This week we begin from the reader's impression: "this paragraph feels fast." Then we look at verbs, sentence length, and punctuation to ask whether any stylistic features support that feeling.

### 3. Review Layer 2

From Week 5 to Week 7, we tried n-grams, co-occurrence, word vectors, and nearby words. In Week 8, we focus less on adding methods and more on asking which method is useful for which kind of question.

## 3. Today's Questions

1. Which paragraph in the story feels fastest to read?
2. Does that paragraph have distinctive verbs, short sentences, punctuation, repetition, or shifts of perspective?
3. Is speed represented as pleasurable skating, or as uncontrollable madness and fear?
4. Do the quantitative features match your reading experience, or do they differ from it?
5. Which Layer 2 method is most useful for this text?

## 4. What We Will Do Today

1. Share comments
2. Review the structure of the story and its descriptions of skating, madness, and speed
3. Compare the "fast paragraphs" chosen by students
4. Observe verbs, sentence length, punctuation, and repetition
5. Ask whether quantitative features match the felt experience of reading
6. Review Layer 2 methods and prepare to move into Layer 3
