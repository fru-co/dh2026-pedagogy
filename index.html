New to Digital Humanities?

This class is designed for students (and a teacher) who are entirely new to Digital Humanities.

Students who have been hesitant or reluctant to use digital methods in their studies—myself included—are especially welcome.

Course context

American literature through three digital lenses

An undergraduate American literature course primarily for English majors with little or no CS or DH background.

Each week, the class reads one short story and experiments with frequency and context, words in relation, and contextual search.

Course materials

Twelve weeks of computational reading, followed by a final-paper workshop

The detailed course syllabus, twelve English weekly handouts, the final paper assignment, and three research-methods handouts document the course design and its progression.

Generative AI experiment

What changes when an AI can use tools?

The experiment began with a practical concern: could a student delegate the entire final paper to generative AI and receive a plausible submission without performing the course’s analytical work? Codex and Claude Code were given the assignment and course materials to test that possibility.

The Claude Code papers directly compare fabricated results with executed analyses, while the Codex paper documents a hybrid workflow combining executed counts, a local approximation, and precomputed embeddings. Each paper is accompanied by notes on its creation conditions.

Reuse and adaptation

Start with a literary question, not code.

The materials can be adapted by substituting a new literary text, choosing one small computational operation, and asking students to interpret both what the result reveals and what it cannot explain.
